üéì Test-Taking Strategies for Part 107

‚è±Ô∏è Time Management (2 Hours for 60 Questions)

  • 2 minutes per question average - You have plenty of time, don't rush
  • Read carefully - Watch for keywords like "EXCEPT," "NOT," "MINIMUM," "MAXIMUM"
  • Flag difficult questions - Skip and return later, don't get stuck
  • First 30 questions in 45 minutes - Leaves 75 minutes for remaining questions and review
  • Save 15 minutes at end - Review flagged questions and check for mistakes

‚úÖ Answer Elimination Strategy

  • Read all options - Don't choose the first "correct-looking" answer
  • Eliminate obviously wrong answers - Cross out impossible options mentally
  • Look for absolutes - Words like "always," "never," "only" are often wrong
  • Compare remaining options - Choose the MOST correct answer
  • Trust your preparation - Your first instinct is usually right

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Exam Traps to Avoid

  • "EXCEPT" questions - Find the answer that is FALSE or doesn't belong
  • AGL vs MSL confusion - Pay attention to whether altitude is above ground or sea level
  • Class D vs E surface areas - Dashed magenta (D) vs magenta shading (E)
  • 400 feet rule variations - Remember structure exception (+400' above structure)
  • Weather minimums - 3 SM visibility and "clear of clouds" for Part 107 (not manned aircraft rules)
  • Night operations - Anti-collision lighting required, visible for 3 statute miles
  • Remote ID compliance - Required for most operations unless specific exceptions apply

üìã Chart & Performance Questions

  • Study the chart legend - Provided on the exam, but know what to look for
  • Airport symbols - Blue = towered, Magenta = non-towered, size indicates runway length
  • MEF (Maximum Elevation Figures) - Large numbers in each chart quadrant
  • Airspace floors - Numbers in fractions (e.g., "40/SFC" means 4,000 MSL to surface)
  • Restricted areas - Red hatched borders (R-XXXX), prohibited areas (P-XXXX)
  • Density altitude calculations - High/hot/humid = worse performance, longer takeoff
  • Wind effect - Headwind = shorter ground track, tailwind = longer ground track

üö´ Common Misconceptions & "Gotcha" Topics

These are the areas where most people make mistakes on the actual exam. Review these carefully:

‚ùå Misconception #1: "Airspace authorization works the same everywhere"

Reality: Class B, C, D, and surface area E require ATC authorization. Class G and Class E above 400' AGL typically don't. But even in Class G, if you're near an airport (within lateral boundaries of surface area controlled airspace), you may still need authorization. Always check B4UFLY or LAANC before any flight.

‚ùå Misconception #2: "400 feet means 400 feet everywhere"

Reality: The 400-foot rule is 400 feet AGL (Above Ground Level), not MSL. Exception: You can fly up to 400 feet above a structure's immediate uppermost limit if you're within 400 feet horizontally of that structure. Questions often test whether you know AGL vs MSL and the structure exception.

‚ùå Misconception #3: "Class D and Class E surface areas look the same on charts"

Reality: Class D has dashed magenta lines, Class E surface areas have magenta shading/vignette. Both require authorization for Part 107 operations. This is a frequently tested topic - memorize the visual differences.

‚ùå Misconception #4: "Part 107 weather minimums are the same as manned aircraft"

Reality: Part 107 requires 3 statute miles visibility and "clear of clouds" (not in or through clouds). This is DIFFERENT from VFR cloud clearance requirements (500 below, 1000 above, 2000 horizontal). Don't confuse Part 107 rules with manned aircraft VFR rules!

‚ùå Misconception #5: "Remote ID is optional"

Reality: Remote ID is currently required for most Part 107 operations unless operating in an FAA-Recognized Identification Area (FRIA) or the drone meets specific exemption criteria (such as being built before the Remote ID compliance deadline and weighing under 0.55 lbs). The exam will test your knowledge of Remote ID compliance requirements and exceptions.

‚ùå Misconception #6: "Density altitude only matters for manned aircraft"

Reality: High density altitude (high/hot/humid conditions) reduces drone performance - less lift, shorter battery life, sluggish controls. Density altitude increases with: higher field elevation, higher temperature, higher humidity, lower barometric pressure. Expect at least 2-3 questions on density altitude effects.

‚ùå Misconception #7: "I can fly at night without any special requirements"

Reality: Night operations (30 minutes after sunset to 30 minutes before sunrise, or civil twilight) require: (1) Anti-collision lighting visible for 3 statute miles, and (2) Completion of updated initial or recurrent training that includes night operations. Night operations questions appear on most exams.

‚ùå Misconception #8: "VLOS means I can see the drone with my naked eye"

Reality: Visual Line of Sight (VLOS) means the remote pilot must be able to see the unmanned aircraft with vision that is unaided by any device other than corrective lenses. This means: No binoculars, no FPV goggles as sole means of vision, no camera zoom. A visual observer can assist, but RPIC must maintain VLOS. VLOS questions often include trick scenarios about FPV or binoculars.

üìú After You Pass: Certification Process

  1. Immediate Results: You'll see your pass/fail status on the screen immediately after completing the exam.
  2. Receive Your Report: The testing center will print a temporary passing certificate and your Airman Knowledge Test Report.
  3. TSA Background Check: Apply online via FAA DroneZone (faadronezone.faa.gov) within 90 days. This includes a TSA security background check.
  4. Processing Time: Typically 6-8 weeks for the FAA to process your application and conduct the background check.
  5. Receive Certificate: Your Part 107 Remote Pilot Certificate will be mailed or available for download via DroneZone.
  6. Certificate Validity: Valid for 24 months (2 years) from the date of issuance.
  7. Recurrent Training: Before expiration, complete online recurrent training (free on FAA Safety website) or retake the exam ($175).

üí° Pro Tip: Apply for your certificate in DroneZone immediately after passing to start the background check process as soon as possible. Don't wait - the sooner you apply, the sooner you can legally fly commercially!
